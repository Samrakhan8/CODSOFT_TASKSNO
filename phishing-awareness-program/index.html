The 9 universal red flags
    Login Pages

    Real Page or Fake Login?

    A phishing link usually leads to a pixel-perfect copy of a login page. The design is fake-proof — but the address bar never lies. Inspect both:

    🔒 https://accounts.google.com/signin
    G

    Sign in

    ✓ Genuine — correct domain, valid certificate
    🔒 https://accounts.google.com.verify-login.ru/signin
    G

    Sign in

    ✗ Fake — the real domain is verify-login.ru
    How to read a URL like a pro

    Read the domain right-to-left from the first single slash. The true owner is the last two labels before that slash:

    https://accounts.google.com.verify-login.ru/signin

    • 🔒 HTTPS / the padlock means nothing. Anyone can get a free certificate — it proves the connection is encrypted, not that the site is honest.
    • Hover over links before clicking to preview the real destination.
    • Type important addresses yourself or use a bookmark — never trust an emailed link.
    • A password manager won't autofill on a look-alike domain. If it doesn't offer to fill, that's a warning.
    Fake Websites

    Fraudulent Websites & Look-alike Domains

    Attackers register domains that look right at a glance. Can you tell the trap from the real thing?

    Common domain tricks
    • Typosquatting — arnazon.com, gooogle.com, micros0ft.com
    • Homoglyphs — swapping letters for look-alikes: paypaI.com (capital i), or Cyrillic characters that render identically to Latin ones.
    • Subdomain deception — apple.com.secure-billing.net (owner is secure-billing.net).
    • Extra words & hyphens — netflix-account-update.com, secure-paypal-login.com.
    • Wrong TLD — yourbank.co instead of .com, or unusual endings like .zip, .tk.
    The Human Factor

    The Psychology of the Con

    Phishing works because it hijacks normal human instincts. These are the six levers criminals pull — click each to learn the play and the defence.

    The golden rule

    Any message that creates pressure to act quickly and discourages you from checking deserves suspicion — no matter who it appears to be from. Slow down. Verify through a channel you trust.

    Daily Habits

    Your Everyday Defence Kit

    Simple habits that stop the vast majority of phishing. Tick them off as you commit to each.

      If you think you've been phished
      1. Don't panic, act fast. Disconnect from the network if malware may have run.
      2. Change your password immediately — from a different, trusted device — and any account that shared it.
      3. Enable / re-check MFA on the affected account.
      4. Report it to your IT / security team right away. Reporting quickly limits the damage — you will not be blamed for reporting.
      5. Watch for follow-ups — attackers often use one compromised account to target others.
